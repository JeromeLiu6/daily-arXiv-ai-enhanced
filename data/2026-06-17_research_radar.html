Tensor-based second-order causal discovery

Nathan Ouyang, Kexin Wan, Anna Seigal

This paper introduces a new algorithm called TSCD that can figure out cause-and-effect relationships between variables by looking at how their covariance matrices change under different conditions (observational data vs. interventional experiments). It works efficiently with hundreds of variables and doesn't require the data to follow a normal distribution.

Tensor-based causal discovery from covariance matricesSecond-order statistics for causal structure learningLinear SEM with uncorrelated noise assumptionNonlinear extension via feature mapsLogarithmic intervention efficiency guarantee
Why this matters

Technical summary

TSCD takes as input a tensor constructed from covariance matrices of both observational and interventional data. It assumes a linear structural equation model on a DAG with uncorrelated noise, and outputs both the causal DAG and the edge functions. The method leverages second-order statistics (covariance matrices) rather than higher-order moments, which provides statistical and computational efficiency. The authors prove identifiability of causal order and parameters from a logarithmic number of interventions relative to the number of variables. A nonlinear extension is also provided. Experiments demonstrate robustness to noise, competitive performance against existing causal discovery methods, and scalability to hundreds of variables.

Why read it

Moderately relevant. Causal discovery is a core methodological interest for Weijie (Mendelian randomisation, drug-target MR). However, this paper is purely methodological with synthetic validation, and the requirement for interventional data limits direct application to typical observational epidemiological settings. The tensor/covariance approach is novel but not yet connected to genetics or proteomics.

Connection to my work

Could inform methodological thinking about causal structure learning from multi-omics data (e.g., protein-protein interaction networks, causal ordering of molecular traits). The logarithmic intervention efficiency is theoretically appealing for designing experiments with limited perturbation data (e.g., CRISPR screens in osteoarthritis models).

Research idea

Adapt TSCD to work with pQTL data as "natural interventions" (genetic variants as instruments) to infer causal ordering among proteins in osteoarthritis-relevant pathways. Compare with existing MR-based causal network methods (e.g., network MR, bi-directional MR) on UK Biobank or All of Us proteomics data.

arXiv 2606.17867v1

A Quantitative Analysis of Multimodal Biomarkers in Alzheimer's Disease

Antonio Scardace, Daniele Ravì

This paper studies Alzheimer's disease by combining different types of data — brain scans (tau-PET and structural MRI), cognitive tests, and genetic information (APOE4) — from 789 people. The authors analyse how these data types relate to each other, which ones provide unique vs. redundant information, and how they track disease progression. They find that tau protein buildup and brain shrinkage are linked but also have independent effects on cognitive decline.

Mutual information analysis for cross-modal redundancy quantificationVariance explained analysis across modalitiesRegion-wise tau-atrophy association mappingStatistical decomposition of tau-cognition association into atrophy-related and atrophy-independent componentsTrajectory analysis of neurodegeneration aligned with cognitive decline
Why this matters

Technical summary

The authors perform a systematic multimodal analysis of AD biomarkers using tau-PET, structural MRI, cognitive scores (MMSE, CDR), and APOE4 genotype from 789 ADNI subjects. They quantify cross-modal redundancy via mutual information and variance explained; examine region-wise associations between tau topology and atrophy; decompose tau-cognition associations into atrophy-related and atrophy-independent components using mediation-style statistical decomposition; and identify a dominant neurodegenerative trajectory aligned with cognitive decline.

Why read it

Moderate. The multimodal integration framework is conceptually relevant to Weijie's interest in multimodal prediction and multi-omics integration for osteoarthritis. However, the paper focuses on Alzheimer's disease with neuroimaging and cognitive biomarkers, not proteomics or OA. The statistical decomposition approach (tau-cognition mediation) could be adapted for OA biomarker pathway analysis.

Connection to my work

The cross-modal redundancy quantification (mutual information, variance explained) could be adapted to assess redundancy among proteomic, metabolomic, and clinical biomarkers in OA. The decomposition method for separating direct vs. mediated biomarker effects could be applied to study how proteomic signatures relate to OA progression through vs. independent of structural imaging changes.

Research idea

Apply similar cross-modal redundancy analysis to UK Biobank OA data: quantify mutual information between plasma proteomics, MRI-based joint structural features, and clinical pain/function scores. Decompose the protein-to-symptom pathway into joint-structure-mediated and independent components, potentially identifying proteins that predict pain independently of structural damage.

arXiv 2606.17600v1

Proximal Mediation Analysis with Hidden Recanting Witnesses

Sihan Wu, Yang Bai, Yifan Cui

This paper develops new statistical methods for mediation analysis — figuring out how much of a treatment's effect goes through a specific intermediate variable (mediator) — when there are hidden confounders that affect both the mediator and the outcome. The authors use a technique called "proximal causal inference" to handle these unobserved confounders (called "recanting witnesses") without needing to measure them directly. They propose three identification strategies and a robust estimation method that works well even if some parts of the statistical model are misspecified.

Proximal causal inferencePath-specific effects (PSEs)Efficient influence function (EIF)Proximal multiply robust (PMR) estimatorDebiased machine learning (DML)Minimax optimizationNegative control variables
Why this matters

Technical summary

This paper addresses the identification and estimation of path-specific effects (PSEs) in mediation analysis when recanting witnesses — treatment-induced mediator-outcome confounders — are unobserved. The authors develop three proximal identification strategies leveraging negative control variables (treatment-induced and outcome-induced proxies) to circumvent the need for observing these confounders. They derive the efficient influence function (EIF) for the identified PSE and propose a proximal multiply robust (PMR) estimator that remains consistent if at least one of the nuisance models (outcome regression, mediator density, or propensity score) is correctly specified. Under correct specification and appropriate convergence rates of all nuisance models, the estimator achieves asymptotic normality and attains the semiparametric efficiency bound. A minimax optimization-based debiased machine learning (DML) procedure is provided for inference. The method is demonstrated on synthetic data and a real data application.

Why read it

Moderate. Mediation analysis is central to understanding causal pathways in epidemiology (e.g., how a drug affects osteoarthritis through protein biomarkers). However, this paper is highly theoretical (statistical methodology) and does not directly address the specific challenges of proteomics, pQTL, or multi-omics data. The proximal causal inference framework could be relevant for drug-target MR where unobserved confounding between protein mediators and outcomes is a concern.

Connection to my work

Could be applied to drug-target Mendelian randomisation studies where one wants to decompose the total effect of a genetic instrument (e.g., pQTL) on an outcome (e.g., osteoarthritis) into direct and indirect pathways through a protein mediator, while accounting for unobserved confounders between the protein and the outcome. The multiply robust property is attractive for real-world data with model uncertainty.

Research idea

Develop a proximal mediation framework for two-sample MR settings with pQTL data, where negative control variables could be derived from independent GWAS summary statistics or from different protein assays. Alternatively, extend the method to handle multiple mediators (e.g., multiple proteins in a proteomics panel) to decompose causal effects in multi-omics mediation analysis for osteoarthritis.

WEQA: Wearable hEalth Question Answering with Query-Adaptive Agentic Reasoning

Yuwei Zhang, Tong Xia, Bianca Emmerich, Yu Yvonne Wu, Dimitris Spathis, Xin Liu, Daniel McDuff, Cecilia Mascolo

This paper presents WEQA, a system that uses a large language model (LLM) as a "controller" to answer questions about wearable health data (like step counts, heart rate, sleep). Instead of trying to make one model do everything, WEQA lets the LLM decide which specialized tools and models to call for each question, then checks the answer against external knowledge. The authors built a benchmark from four wearable datasets and showed their system is 24% more accurate than standard LLM approaches, with medical experts rating it higher for usefulness and clinical soundness.

Query-adaptive agentic reasoningLLM controller with dynamic execution planningTool-augmented LLM for wearable data analysisGrounded response auditing with external knowledgeMulti-dataset benchmark for wearable health QA
Why this matters

Technical summary

WEQA is a query-adaptive agentic framework for wearable health data question answering. It uses an LLM controller to dynamically generate execution plans, routing each query to appropriate sensor analysis tools and pretrained models (e.g., time-series classifiers, regression models). The framework includes grounded response auditing using external knowledge bases. The authors curated a benchmark from four open wearable datasets (e.g., UK Biobank accelerometry, Fitbit, Apple Watch) covering analytic (e.g., "what is my average HR?") and predictive (e.g., "will I get sick?") tasks across three health domains (activity, sleep, cardiovascular). Experiments compare against LLM-only, agentic (ReAct, Reflexion), and fine-tuned baselines. A blinded study with 12 medical experts and 8 users assessed clinical soundness and usefulness.

Why read it

Tangential. The paper focuses on wearable data QA rather than proteomics, pQTL, multi-omics, or osteoarthritis. However, the agentic LLM framework concept could be adapted for multi-omics query answering or clinical prediction tasks. The use of UK Biobank accelerometry data is a minor connection.

Connection to my work

The agentic framework design could inspire a system for querying multi-omics data (proteomics + genomics + EHR) for osteoarthritis risk prediction. The idea of dynamic tool routing could be applied to selecting between different PRS methods, MR estimators, or ML models depending on the research question.

Research idea

Develop a query-adaptive agent for osteoarthritis multi-omics research: an LLM controller that routes user queries (e.g., "which proteins mediate obesity's effect on OA risk?") to appropriate tools (pQTL MR, colocalization, proteomic-wide association, PRS analysis) and synthesizes results with grounded citations. Could be extended to multimodal prediction combining proteomics, genetics, and wearable data.

Vision-language models for chest radiography do not always need the image

Mahshad Lotfinia, Sebastian Ziegelmayer, Lisa Adams, Daniel Truhn, Andreas Maier, Soroosh Tayebi Arasteh

This paper shows that many vision-language models for chest X-ray analysis appear to perform well not because they actually "see" the image, but because they exploit statistical patterns in the text labels (e.g., knowing which findings commonly co-occur). The authors designed a causal audit that tests whether a model's correct answer depends on the image by occluding relevant/irrelevant regions or swapping in a different patient's scan. They found that a text-only model (no image) performed nearly as well as the best multimodal model, and a huge 119-billion-parameter multimodal model was indistinguishable from a tiny 7-billion text-only baseline. The audit revealed that many models essentially ignore the image. The authors argue that accuracy benchmarks alone are misleading and that grounding audits should be required before clinical deployment.

Causal audit framework with three image interventions (relevant occlusion, irrelevant occlusion, same-label swap)Behavioral metrics for grounding assessmentComparison of text-only vs multimodal VLMsStatistical indistinguishability testing between models and radiologistsConfidence-based ungrounded answer flagging
Why this matters

Technical summary

The paper introduces a causal auditing framework for medical vision-language models (VLMs) that intervenes on the image input via three perturbations: (1) occluding the clinically relevant region, (2) occluding an irrelevant region, and (3) swapping in another patient's scan with the same label. Three behavioral metrics are combined to test whether correct predictions depend on image content. Nine systems were evaluated, including multimodal VLMs and a text-only baseline (no image access). The text-only model reached within 5.7 accuracy points of the best multimodal model. A 119B-parameter multimodal model was statistically indistinguishable from a 7B text-only baseline. The audit stratified models into three categories: image-ignoring (3 models), unstable (1 model), and selective image-using (5 models, for a subset of findings). Against board-certified radiologists, the text-only model matched radiologist accuracy but with zero grounding, while image-using models achieved radiologist-comparable grounding rates. Confidence scores flagged ungrounded answers only when the model actually used the image.

Why read it

Low direct relevance. This paper is about vision-language models for chest X-rays, not about proteomics, pQTL, multi-omics, or osteoarthritis. However, the methodological insight about causal auditing of multimodal models is broadly relevant to Weijie's interest in multimodal prediction (e.g., combining imaging, EHR, proteomics, wearables). The core lesson—that accuracy benchmarks can be misleading when models exploit label priors—is important for any multimodal clinical prediction work.

Connection to my work

The causal auditing framework could inspire similar validation approaches for multimodal prediction models in osteoarthritis (e.g., combining MRI/X-ray with proteomics and clinical data). Before deploying a multimodal OA risk model, one should verify that it actually uses the imaging data and not just strong proteomic or demographic priors.

Research idea

Develop a causal grounding audit for multimodal osteoarthritis prediction models that combine imaging (X-ray/MRI) with proteomics and EHR data. Test whether the model actually uses imaging features or relies primarily on proteomic signatures and clinical covariates. This could be a methodological contribution to the OA multimodal prediction literature.
